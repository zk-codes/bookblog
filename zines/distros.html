Historical Distribution Models

Mail Order and the Postal System

For decades, the US Postal Service (and postal systems globally) were the infrastructure that made zine culture possible. Before the internet, if you wanted to read a zine from someone in another city, you mailed them cash or a check, and they mailed you a zine. Simple, slow, and absolutely vital.

Zinesters would list their addresses in their zines, in Factsheet Five, in other zines' "distro" sections. You'd write a letter, include money, maybe include a personal note, and wait. A few weeks later, a zine would arrive in your mailbox. That physical object traveling across the country felt significant in a way that downloading a file never quite does.

The Romance of Mail Order

There was something magical about mail-order zine culture. Every trip to the mailbox might contain a new zine from someone you'd never met. You'd learn about people's lives through their zines and the letters they included. Pen-pal relationships formed. People met up when they traveled to each other's cities. The slowness and physicality of it created different kinds of relationships than instant digital communication does. Some zinesters still prefer mail order for exactly this reason—it filters for people who care enough to go through the effort.

Mail order had its problems, though. Zinesters would send money and never receive zines. Zines would get lost in the mail. International shipping was expensive and unreliable. But overall, the system worked because it was based on trust and the assumption that most people were decent.

Record Stores, Bookstores, and Independent Shops

Physical retail was another major distribution channel. Independent record stores, especially punk and alternative shops, often carried zines on consignment. Same with independent bookstores, coffee shops, comic shops, and any other independently-owned space whose proprietors were sympathetic to DIY culture.

The arrangement was usually informal: you'd bring in a stack of zines, leave them with the store, come back periodically to restock and collect money. The store would take 30-50% of the cover price. It wasn't particularly profitable for anyone, but it got zines in front of people who might not seek them out otherwise.

This model has largely declined as independent retail has struggled. Record stores and bookstores have closed or cut back on carrying zines. The ones that still carry them are treasures and should be supported.

Distros: The Middleman Model

Zine distros (short for "distributors") emerged as a way to scale up distribution without losing the DIY ethos. A distro would carry dozens or hundreds of zines from different creators, sell them via mail order or at events, and send the creators their share of the profits.

Some distros were businesses, some were more like personal projects. The good ones actively promoted the zines they carried, wrote descriptions, built relationships with customers. The bad ones sat on inventory, were terrible at paying creators, or just disappeared with people's money and zines.

Distros were especially important for zinesters who didn't want to deal with the logistics of mail order themselves—the addressing envelopes, the trips to the post office, the record-keeping. You could focus on making zines and let someone else handle distribution.

Footnote on Distro Economics: Distros typically took 40-60% of a zine's cover price. This sounds like a lot, but consider: they're handling storage, shipping, promotion, customer service, and financial risk (they buy inventory upfront). The markup is similar to traditional retail. Still, many zinesters resented giving up that much of the profit, especially when a $3 zine cost maybe 50 cents to produce. The tension between DIY ideals and economic reality is eternal.

Contemporary Distribution: The Zine Fest Era

The Rise of Zine Fests

Zine fests—events where zinesters gather to sell, trade, and celebrate zines—have become the primary distribution method in the 21st century. They started small in the 1990s and exploded in the 2000s and 2010s. Now almost every major city has at least one annual zine fest, and many have multiple events throughout the year.

The format is simple: zinesters rent tables (usually $10-30), set up their wares, and spend a day or weekend selling directly to readers. Some fests have hundreds of tables, thousands of attendees, and programming like workshops, readings, and performances. Others are tiny—a dozen tables in a library meeting room.

Major zine fests include: LA Zine Fest (one of the largest), Portland Zine Symposium, New York Art Book Fair, Chicago Zine Fest, San Francisco Zine Fest, and dozens of others. Each has its own character—some are more art-focused, some more punk, some more literary, some wonderfully eclectic.

Why Zine Fests Exploded

Zine fests address several problems that other distribution methods couldn't solve. They create face-to-face community in an increasingly digital world. They let zinesters sell directly without middlemen taking a cut. They're accessible—anyone can get a table. They're events, not just transactions. And they generate energy and inspiration—you see what other people are making, you get ideas, you remember why you started making zines in the first place. Plus, they're incredibly fun if you enjoy nerding out about zines for hours.

How Zine Fests Work

Most zine fests are organized by volunteers, sometimes with institutional support from libraries or art centers. They're often non-profit or break-even ventures—table fees cover venue rental, promotion, and maybe a little money for organizers.

If you want to table at a zine fest, you typically register in advance (some fests sell out quickly), pay your fee, show up early on the day to set up, and then spend the day talking to people about your zines. Bring change, bring a tablecloth, bring business cards or zines with your contact info. Be friendly. Don't be pushy. Let people browse.

As an attendee, you walk around, look at tables, flip through zines that interest you, buy ones you want. Most zines at fests are still priced between $2-6. Bring small bills. Talk to creators if you want, but respect their energy—they've been talking all day. Some people go to zine fests to buy, some to trade, some just to see what's happening.

Aside: Zine Fest Etiquette Don't just photograph people's tables and walk away—it's rude. If you read a whole zine at someone's table and don't buy it, that's borderline. Don't complain about prices to the creator—they set prices based on their costs and labor. Do trade if you're a zinester—most people love trades. Do tell creators specifically what you liked about their work. Do buy zines even if you could read the same content online for free—you're supporting the creator and getting a physical object. Don't be a jerk. It's a small community.

Online Distribution and Social Media

The Internet Changed Everything (But Not the Way We Expected)

When the internet became widely accessible in the late 1990s, everyone predicted zines would move online and physical zines would disappear. What actually happened was more complicated. Some zinesters did move online, starting blogs and websites. But many kept making physical zines and used the internet as a promotional and ordering tool.

Now most zinesters have some online presence—Instagram accounts showing new releases, Etsy or Big Cartel shops for online ordering, websites with zine info and contact details. The internet didn't replace physical zines; it became another tool for reaching readers and building community.

Social Media as Discovery

Instagram especially has become huge for zine culture. Zinesters post photos of their zines, work-in-progress shots, table setups at fests. Readers discover new zines by following hashtags like #zine #zinesofinstagram #perzine. It's a visual medium, which works well for showing off zine aesthetics.

But social media has its problems. Algorithms decide who sees your posts. Platforms change their rules. You don't own your content or your audience—if Instagram deleted your account tomorrow, you'd lose all those connections. This has made some zinesters wary of depending too much on social media for distribution and community.

Footnote on Platform Dependence: The tension between using social media effectively and not becoming dependent on corporate platforms is ongoing. Many zinesters try to use social media to drive people to their websites, newsletters, or IRL presence rather than making Instagram the primary way people engage with their work. The goal is to use the tools without letting the tools use you.

Online Shops and Distros

Many distros now operate primarily online. Big Cartel, Etsy, and Storenvy are popular platforms for selling zines. Some zinesters run their own websites with shopping cart functionality. PayPal, Venmo, and other payment systems have made online transactions easier than mailing cash ever was.

Online distros can reach a global audience in a way physical distros never could. Someone in Australia can order a zine from someone in Ohio without either of them leaving their house. The trade-off is that shipping costs can be prohibitive for international orders, and the whole transaction feels less personal than the old mail-order model.

Libraries and Archives

An increasingly important part of zine distribution is preservation through libraries and archives. Many libraries now have zine collections—not just research libraries, but public libraries. They collect zines, catalog them, and make them available to patrons.

This serves multiple functions: it gives zines institutional legitimacy, it preserves ephemeral culture that might otherwise be lost, and it makes zines accessible to people who can't afford to buy them or don't have access to zine fests. It also creates a historical record for researchers.

Notable Zine Libraries and Archives

Barnard Zine Library (New York) has one of the largest and most accessible zine collections, focusing on feminist and gender-related zines. The Denver Zine Library operates as a lending library. The NYC Feminist Zine Fest maintains an archive. Queer Zine Archive Project preserves LGBTQ+ zines. Many universities have special collections that include zines. See Resources & Archives for a more complete list.

Donating to zine libraries is a good way to ensure your work survives and reaches new audiences. Most zine libraries accept donations and will catalog and preserve them properly. It's also a way to get your work in front of researchers, students, and people who discover zines through libraries rather than through scenes.

The Economics of Zine Distribution

Let's talk money, because someone has to. Zine distribution is generally not profitable. Most zinesters lose money or barely break even when you factor in time and materials. This isn't a path to financial success.

The Math

Say you make a 24-page half-size zine. Printing costs maybe 50 cents per copy if you're photocopying, maybe less if you have access to free copies. You price it at $3, which is standard. If you sell through a distro or shop, they take 40-50%, so you get $1.50-1.80 per copy. Minus your production costs, you're making $1-1.30 per zine. This doesn't account for your labor—the hours spent creating, assembling, promoting, shipping.

At a zine fest, you keep all the money, but you pay a table fee ($20-30 typically) and transportation costs. If you sell 20 zines at $3 each, you make $60, minus $30 for the table, minus $20 for photocopies, minus gas money or public transit fare. You might clear $20 for a full day of labor. Again, not lucrative.

Aside: Why Do People Do This? If it's not profitable, why bother? Because money isn't the only currency. You get creative satisfaction, community, self-expression, the joy of making physical objects, relationships with readers, the feeling of participating in something bigger than yourself. Also, for many people, not everything has to be monetized. Sometimes making art is enough. The capitalism-brain that demands everything be profitable is the enemy of creativity.

Strategies for Sustainability

If you want to keep making zines without going broke, here are some approaches:

Keep production costs low. Use free copying if you can access it ethically. Print smaller runs. Use cheaper paper. The DIY aesthetic embraces low production values anyway.

Sell directly when possible. Zine fests and mail order let you keep all the profit instead of splitting it with middlemen.

Price realistically. Don't underprice out of misplaced modesty. If your zine cost you $1 to make and took you 20 hours to create, $5 is not too much to charge.

Do trades instead of or alongside sales. Building a zine collection through trades costs no money and builds community.

Accept that this is a hobby, not a business. If you go in expecting to make money, you'll be disappointed. If you go in expecting to make something you're proud of and connect with like-minded people, you'll probably succeed.

Regional Differences in Zine Scenes

Zine culture looks different in different places. Some cities have thriving, interconnected scenes. Others have isolated pockets of zinesters who barely know each other exist. Geography, local culture, and infrastructure all matter.

Urban vs. Rural

Urban zine scenes tend to be larger, more visible, and better connected. Cities have the density to support zine fests, shops that carry zines, and enough zinesters to form communities. There's more cross-pollination between zine culture and other DIY scenes—music, art, activism.

Rural and small-town zinesters often work in more isolation. They might not have access to zine fests, shops, or other zinesters locally. The internet has helped—you can be part of a zine scene online even if you're physically isolated. But there's still something about in-person community that's hard to replicate digitally.

The Portland Effect

Some cities are disproportionately influential in zine culture. Portland, Oregon especially has been a zine mecca since the 1990s—large, active scene, multiple zine fests annually, supportive infrastructure. Why Portland? Partly historical accident (punk and DIY scenes were already strong), partly affordability (it used to be cheap to live there), partly self-perpetuating (people moved there for the zine scene, which strengthened the scene). Other cities with strong scenes include Los Angeles, New York, San Francisco, Chicago, Seattle. But scenes can emerge anywhere if a few dedicated people put in the work.

International Scenes

Zine culture is global, though US and UK zines have historically been most visible in English-language documentation. Canada, Australia, Mexico, Brazil, Japan, South Korea, and many European countries all have active zine scenes with their own characteristics and traditions.

Language is obviously a barrier for international distribution—most zines circulate within their linguistic communities. But visual zines (art, comics) cross language barriers more easily. International zine fests and mail trading have created some cross-border connections.

Digital distribution has made international access easier. You can now discover a zine from Seoul or São Paulo and order it online. Shipping is expensive, but it's possible. The global zine scene is more interconnected than ever, even as it remains rooted in local communities.

Building and Maintaining Scenes

Zine scenes don't just exist—they're created and maintained by people putting in work. If you want a zine scene where you live, you might have to be the person who makes it happen.

Starting a Scene

Make zines yourself. You can't have a scene with no zines. Start by creating and distributing your own work.

Find other zinesters. They exist, even in small towns, but they might be invisible. Post on local social media, put up flyers, ask at independent bookstores and coffee shops. Once you find one other zinester, you can find more through connections.

Create infrastructure. Organize a zine meetup at a coffee shop or library. Start a zine-swap night. If there's interest, organize a small zine fest—it doesn't have to be huge to be valuable. Offer to teach a zine-making workshop. Convince a local shop to carry zines on consignment.

Be consistent. Scenes need regular activity to thrive. A one-time event might generate interest, but regular meetups or annual events give people something to build around.

Footnote on Scene Politics: Wherever humans gather, politics emerge. Zine scenes are no exception. Conflicts arise over who's included, whose work gets promoted, what the scene's values are, who gets credit for building the scene. This is exhausting and inevitable. Try to be inclusive, give credit generously, address problems directly rather than letting them fester, and remember that scenes are made of humans with all their flaws. Perfection is impossible; good-enough community is achievable.

Keeping Scenes Healthy

Established scenes face their own challenges. They can become cliquish, excluding newcomers. They can lose energy as original organizers burn out. They can become dominated by one aesthetic or perspective.

Welcome newcomers. Every scene needs new blood. Make it easy for people to find out about events and participate. Don't assume everyone knows the history or the unwritten rules.

Share organizing labor. If the same three people organize everything, they'll burn out. Distribute responsibility. Train new organizers. Make it a collective effort.

Stay diverse. Encourage different kinds of zines, different aesthetics, different perspectives. A scene that's all one type of zine is boring and fragile.

Address problems. If someone's being harmful—harassing people, stealing, making the scene unsafe—deal with it. Ignoring problems in the name of community harmony usually makes things worse.

The Future of Zine Distribution

Predicting the future is foolish, but we can identify trends. Physical zines aren't going anywhere—if anything, they're having a renaissance as people seek alternatives to algorithmically-mediated digital content. But how they're distributed will keep evolving.

Zine fests will likely remain central as long as people value in-person community. Online distribution will continue to grow and improve. Libraries and archives will become increasingly important for preservation and access. New technologies (better printing, new binding methods, whatever comes next) will create new possibilities.

What won't change is the fundamental dynamic: people making things they care about and finding ways to share them with other people. The specific mechanisms might shift, but the impulse is constant. As long as people have something to say and want to say it outside corporate or institutional channels, there will be zines. And wherever there are zines, there will be people figuring out how to get them into each other's hands.

Zine Scenes & Distribution

Zines don't exist in isolation. They're objects that move through networks of people, creating communities and conversations. Understanding how zines get distributed and how zine scenes operate is understanding how DIY culture actually functions—not through corporate channels or algorithms, but through trust, reciprocity, and the postal system.

What Is a Zine Scene?

A "scene" in zine culture is a network of people making, reading, trading, and talking about zines. Scenes can be geographic (the Portland zine scene), topic-based (the poetry zine scene), or identity-based (the queer zine scene). They can be tightly-knit or loosely connected. What defines a scene is shared interest and communication—people who know about each other's work and interact around it.

Zine scenes overlap constantly. Someone might be part of their local city's zine scene, the national riot grrrl zine scene, and the international art zine scene all at once. The boundaries are fluid, and people move between scenes as their interests and locations change.

Aside: Scenes vs. Communities "Scene" and "community" aren't quite synonymous. A scene is about production and circulation—who's making what and how it's moving around. A community is about relationships and support. Many zine scenes are also communities, but some scenes are more competitive or disconnected. The best zine scenes feel like communities where people support each other's work, but don't assume every scene is a warm fuzzy family.
