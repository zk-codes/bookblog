Making Zines

Here's the beautiful secret about making zines: if you can operate a photocopier and a stapler, you can make a zine. Everything else is just details, options, and personal preference. This page will walk you through the whole process from concept to distribution, but don't let the length intimidate you. People have made excellent zines with far less knowledge than what's collected here.

Aside: Perfection is the Enemy The single biggest obstacle to making a zine is waiting until you know how to do it "right" or until your idea is fully formed. Stop waiting. Make something messy and imperfect. The charm of zines is often in their rough edges, their typos, their weird layout choices. Some of the best zines ever made look like they were assembled during a tornado. Just start.

Step One: What's Your Zine About?

Before you worry about paper weights and binding methods, figure out what you're trying to make. Do you want to write about your life? Document a local scene? Share poetry? Rant about politics? Create an art object? Teach people how to do something? Mix all of the above? There are no wrong answers here.

Some questions that might help:

Who's your audience? Are you making this for your friends, for a specific community, or for whoever stumbles across it? This affects your tone, your references, how much context you provide. A zine for other people in your punk scene can assume shared knowledge. A zine for a general audience needs to explain more.

How personal do you want to get? Perzines (see Types & Genres) thrive on intimacy and honesty, but you get to decide how much of yourself to reveal. Some people use pseudonyms. Some people lay out their entire messy lives. Both approaches are valid.

Is this ongoing or one-shot? Many zines are intended as ongoing publications—issue 1, issue 2, etc. Others are standalone projects. Neither is better, but knowing which you're making helps with planning. If it's ongoing, you probably want a consistent format and title. If it's one-shot, you can go wild.

The Tyranny of the Theme

Some zine makers stress about finding the perfect unifying theme. Here's permission to not care about that. Some of the best zines are gloriously disjointed—a poem, then a rant about your job, then some photos, then a recipe, then more poems. If everything in your head connects for you, that's theme enough. Your consciousness is the unifying factor.

Step Two: Creating Content

Now you actually have to make the thing. This is where people get stuck, so let's break it down into manageable pieces.

Writing

If your zine involves text (most do), just write like you talk. Academic prose is the enemy of good zine writing. So is trying to sound "literary" or "professional." The best zine writing is conversational, direct, and honest. Imagine you're explaining this to a friend over coffee. Write that way.

Don't self-censor too hard on the first draft. Get everything out, then edit. You can always cut later. Many zinesters write way more than they'll use, then select the best parts. Others write exactly to length. Find your process.

Footnote on Editing: Yes, you should edit. No, your zine doesn't need to be perfectly polished, but at minimum, read it out loud to yourself. You'll catch awkward phrasing, repetition, and sentences that don't actually make sense. Even punk zines benefit from basic editing. Intentional roughness is cool; unreadable confusion is not.

Art, Photos, and Illustrations

Not everyone can draw, and that's fine. There are plenty of ways to add visual interest without illustration skills:

Collage is the zinesters' secret weapon. Cut images from magazines, printed photos, found materials. Glue them down. Photocopy the result. Instant visual interest, zero drawing ability required. Be mindful of copyright if you plan to sell your zine (more on that below).

Photos work great in zines. Black and white photocopies of photos have a specific aesthetic that's very zine. Take your own photos, use ones friends give you permission to use, or use very old photos that are in the public domain.

Hand-drawn elements don't have to be good. Crude drawings, doodles, decorative borders, little illustrations—it all adds personality. The DIY aesthetic celebrates amateur art. If you can draw a stick figure, you can illustrate a zine.

Typography and layout are visual elements too. Playing with fonts, sizes, text placement, and white space can make even text-only pages visually interesting. See layout section below.

Aside: Digital Tools for the Analog Zine Many zinesters now create content digitally (type in Word, draw in Photoshop or Procreate, design in InDesign or free alternatives like Scribus) and then print for photocopying. This gives you more control and makes editing easier. But plenty of people still do cut-and-paste layout with physical materials, and that has its own appeal. Use whatever tools you have access to and feel comfortable with.

Layout and Design

Layout is where your content becomes a zine. You're deciding what goes on which page, how text and images relate, what the reader experiences as they move through your publication.

The most common zine format is the half-size zine—you fold standard 8.5" × 11" paper in half to create a booklet of 5.5" × 8.5" pages. See Technical Reference for folding diagrams and other formats. This format is cheap, familiar, and easy to produce.

When laying out pages, remember that they'll be read in a specific order. Page flow matters. You can create rhythm—dense pages followed by sparse ones, text pages followed by image pages. Or you can create chaos—that's valid too.

Margins are important. Leave space around the edges, especially on the spine side where the staples go. Text too close to the spine becomes unreadable. Text running off the edge of the page looks less "intentionally raw" and more "I don't know what I'm doing."

Readability matters more than you think. Yes, zines can have weird layouts and experimental typography, but if your text is actually unreadable, people won't read it. Make sure there's enough contrast between text and background. Make sure your font is big enough. Test by photocopying—sometimes what looks fine on screen becomes a muddy mess when copied.

The Eight-Page Mini-Zine: Perfect for Beginners

If you're intimidated by making a full zine, start with a mini-zine made from a single sheet of paper. You fold and cut it in a specific way to create an eight-page booklet. It's quick, it's cheap, and it's a great way to learn the basics. There are tutorials all over the internet—search "one-page zine folding." You can make one in an afternoon, complete it, and feel the satisfaction of having finished something. Then make more.

Step Three: Printing and Production

You've created your content and laid it out. Now you need to turn it into physical copies. You have options.

Photocopying: The Classic Method

This is how most zines have been made since the 1970s. Take your master pages to a copy shop (or use your work/school copier if you're sneaky and don't mind the ethical ambiguity), copy them, fold them, staple them. Done.

Pros: Cheap, fast, accessible, gives you that classic zine look, easy to do small runs, you can do it the same day you finish layout.

Cons: Limited to black and white (or expensive color copies), quality varies depending on the copier, each copy has slight differences, large runs get tedious.

Footnote on Copy Shop Ethics: Many zinesters have a complicated relationship with copy shops. When you're broke and making something for your community, the idea of paying corporate rates for photocopying feels wrong. Some people use stolen copy codes, make copies on workplace machines, or find other workarounds. We're not going to tell you what to do, but be aware that these actions have consequences if you're caught. Weigh your options and your ethics accordingly.

Home Printing

If you have a printer, you can print at home. This gives you complete control and privacy, but it gets expensive fast if you're making more than a few copies. Ink costs add up. On the other hand, you can print whenever inspiration strikes, even at 3am in your pajamas.

Laser printers are better for zine production than inkjet if you have the option—faster, cheaper per page, doesn't smudge. But inkjet is fine for small runs.

Risograph Printing

Risograph is a Japanese printing technology that's become hugely popular in the zine world over the last decade. It creates vibrant, slightly imperfect prints in limited colors. The aesthetic is distinctive—flat colors, occasional registration misalignment, a kind of vintage-modern look.

Pros: Beautiful color printing, relatively affordable for medium runs, distinctive aesthetic, increasingly available at art centers and makerspaces.

Cons: Not as accessible as photocopiers, requires some technical knowledge, best for runs of 50+, need to design specifically for the medium.

Many cities now have risograph studios that offer access to machines, either through membership or per-job rates. If you're interested in color printing and you're making enough copies to justify it, risograph is worth exploring. See Resources for risograph studios and tutorials.

Digital Printing

For larger runs or if you want higher quality color printing, digital printing (the kind you'd get at a print shop or through an online service) is an option. This is how you get zines that look more like small books.

Pros: High quality, can do full color affordably, consistent results, good for larger runs.

Cons: More expensive per copy for small runs, less DIY aesthetic, requires more planning and design work.

Aside: Print Runs and Economics How many copies should you make? For your first zine, start small—maybe 25-50 copies. You can always make more if you run out. It's better to need a second printing than to have boxes of unsold zines under your bed for years. As you learn your audience and distribution channels, you'll get a better sense of appropriate print runs. Some zinesters do tiny runs of 10-20 for very personal work. Others do hundreds for popular titles.

Step Four: Assembly

Your pages are printed. Now they need to become zines. This is the physical labor part, and it can be meditative or mind-numbing depending on your personality and run size.

Folding

If you're making a folded zine (most common), you need to fold your pages. For small runs, you can fold by hand—crease each fold sharply, maybe use a bone folder (a bookbinding tool) or the edge of a ruler to get crisp folds.

For larger runs, you'll want a system. Some people fold over the edge of a table. Some use a paper folding tool. Some recruit friends and make it a folding party with beer and music.

Binding

Stapling is the standard zine binding method. Two or three staples through the spine, and you're done. Long-reach staplers make this easier—they let you reach the center of the folded pages. You can also unfold the pages, place them on a surface, and staple from the outside, then fold back. See Technical Reference for binding techniques.

Sewing is an alternative that's gaining popularity. Thread or dental floss through punched holes. It's more time-consuming but looks beautiful and holds up better. Plus, no special tools required except a needle and something to punch holes with.

Other binding methods include: saddle stitching (professional sewing), perfect binding (glued spine like a paperback), spiral binding, or just leaving pages loose and held together with a rubber band or paper clip. All valid depending on your aesthetic and the effect you want.

The Ritual of Assembly

Many zinesters report that the assembly process, while tedious, is also strangely satisfying. There's something about the repetitive physical labor of folding and stapling that creates a different relationship to your work than just clicking "publish" on a blog. You're literally putting these things together with your hands. Some people listen to music or podcasts. Some recruit friends. Some use it as meditation time. Find what works for you, because if you keep making zines, you'll be doing a lot of assembly.

Step Five: Distribution

You've made your zines. Now what? They need to find readers. Distribution is its own art form, and there's a whole page about it here, but here are the basics:

Direct Sales

Zine fests are the main way zinesters sell directly to readers now. You get a table, spread out your zines, and people browse and buy. It's social, it's immediate, and you get to meet your readers. Most zine fests charge a small table fee.

Local shops like independent bookstores, record stores, coffee shops, and art spaces will sometimes carry zines on consignment. You leave copies, they sell them, you split the profit. The shop takes 30-50% typically. Good for getting your zine in front of people who aren't specifically seeking zines.

Friends and community are your first audience. Give copies to people who you think will appreciate them. Sell or trade at local shows, events, gatherings. Word of mouth is powerful.

Mail Order

Zine culture has always been mail-order friendly. People still browse zine distros online, find a zine that interests them, and mail cash or PayPal payment for a copy. The romance of finding a personal zine in your mailbox hasn't died.

You can sell directly—announce your zine on social media or a website, have people contact you, mail copies. Or you can work with distros (more on that below).

Footnote on Pricing: Most zines are priced between $2-$6, with $3-$4 being most common for a standard-sized zine. Price based on your production costs plus a little extra for your labor, but don't overprice. Zines are meant to be accessible. Some people do "pay what you can" or free distribution. There's no wrong way to price if your goals are clear. If you're trying to cover costs and make a little money for more supplies, price accordingly. If you're trying to get your work out to as many people as possible, price low or free.

Distros

Zine distros are individuals or small operations that sell zines on consignment. They'll carry your zine on their website or at events, and send you money when copies sell. It's less profitable than selling direct (distros typically take 40-60%), but it gets your zine in front of more people without you doing all the work.

Finding good distros requires research. Look for ones that carry similar work to yours, have a good reputation for paying zinester, and actually sell stuff. See Resources for current distros.

Trading

Zine trading is its own economy. You make a zine, someone else makes a zine, you trade. No money changes hands, but you both get to read something new and you build community. Many zinesters prefer trading to selling—it feels more aligned with the DIY ethos.

Announce on social media that you're open to trades. Connect with other zinesters. Mail zines back and forth. Some people have massive trade networks and never buy zines, just trade for them.

Copyright and Content Warning: Legally speaking, if you use other people's copyrighted images or text (magazine clippings, song lyrics, whatever), you're probably violating copyright. For small personal zines that you're giving away or selling for peanuts, this almost never becomes an issue—you're not worth pursuing legally. But be aware of the risk, especially if your zine becomes popular or if you're using really recognizable copyrighted material. If you're worried, use public domain images, your own photos and art, or get permission. Also, consider including content warnings if your zine deals with heavy topics—not required, but considerate to readers who might need to know what they're getting into.

Step Six: Keep Going (or Don't)

You made a zine. Congratulations, seriously. That's more than most people do. Now you have choices.

Make more issues. If you positioned this as issue 1, people will wonder about issue 2. Ongoing zines build audience over time. You can establish a schedule (quarterly, bi-annually, whenever you feel like it) or just release new issues when they're ready.

Make something completely different. Maybe this zine was about one thing, and your next zine will be about something else entirely. That's fine. You're not locked into a topic or format just because you made one zine.

Collaborate. Team up with other zinesters for anthology zines, split zines (you each do half), or collaborative projects. Working with others brings new energy and ideas, plus splits the labor.

Take a break. Making zines requires energy, time, and often money. It's okay to make one and then not make another for a year. Or ever. There's no obligation to become a serial zinester just because you made one. Your zine exists. That's enough.

Dealing with Criticism (or Lack of Response)

Here's a hard truth: you might make a zine and get very little response. Or someone might tell you they didn't like it. This is difficult, especially if you poured your heart into it. Remember that zines are not optimized for maximum engagement like social media. The response is slower, quieter, more personal. One person telling you at a zine fest that your zine resonated with them is worth a hundred Instagram likes. But also, sometimes your zine won't find its audience right away. That doesn't mean it's bad or that you failed. Keep making things. The right people will find them eventually.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Making it too long. Your first zine doesn't need to be 100 pages. Start small. An 8-page mini-zine or 16-page half-size zine is plenty. You can always make a longer one next time.

Waiting for perfection. Already covered this, but it bears repeating. Done is better than perfect. Release your zine when it's good enough, not when it's flawless.

Bad photocopying. Test your pages before you make 50 copies. Make sure everything is legible, nothing is cut off, the contrast is good. One test copy can save you from wasting money on an unreadable print run.

Forgetting contact info. Put your name (or pseudonym), email, website, or some way for people to contact you in your zine. If someone loves it and wants more, they need to know how to find you.

Overthinking it. The best way to learn to make zines is to make zines. Your first one will be flawed. That's fine. Make it anyway. You'll learn more from completing one imperfect zine than from endlessly planning a perfect one that never gets made.
