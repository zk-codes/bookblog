Glossary

Zine culture has its own vocabulary—jargon, slang, technical terms, and subcultural language. This glossary defines the terms you'll encounter in zines, at zine fests, and in zine communities. Some terms are technical, some are cultural, some are both. Knowing the language helps you participate in the culture, but don't let unfamiliar terms intimidate you—zine culture is welcoming to newcomers who ask questions.

Aside: Language Evolution Like all living language, zine terminology changes. New terms emerge, old terms fall out of use, meanings shift. This glossary reflects usage as of the mid-2020s, but language will continue evolving. When in doubt, ask zinesters what they mean by a term—definitions aren't always universal.

General Zine Terms

Zine (rhymes with "bean" or "fine," both pronunciations are common)
Short for magazine or fanzine. A self-published, small-circulation publication, usually photocopied and DIY in aesthetic. Can be about anything, in any format, as long as it's independently produced and distributed outside commercial channels. See History & Culture for origins.
Zinester
A person who makes zines. The term emphasizes the activity and identity of zine-making. Some people prefer "zine maker" as less scene-y, but "zinester" is widely used and understood.
Fanzine
The original term: fan magazine. Zines created by fans about topics they're enthusiastic about—originally science fiction, later music, then expanded to any topic-focused zine. "Zine" evolved from "fanzine" but now has broader meaning. See History & Culture.
Perzine
Personal zine. A zine focused on the creator's own life, experiences, thoughts, and observations rather than on an external topic. The dominant form of contemporary zines. More in Types & Genres.
Issue
A single publication in an ongoing series. "Issue 5" means the fifth zine in a continuing publication. Some zines are one-shots (single issues), others are ongoing series.
One-Shot
A zine intended as a standalone publication rather than part of an ongoing series. Many zines are one-shots—made once, about a specific topic or project, not intended to continue.
Mini-Zine
A small zine, typically quarter-size or smaller. Often refers specifically to the one-page/eight-page folded format. Quick to make, cheap to produce, good for beginners. See Technical Reference for folding instructions.
Half-Size
The standard zine format: 5.5" × 8.5", created by folding letter-sized paper in half. Most common size for zines. Portable and familiar. More in Technical Reference.

Production and Technical Terms

Cut-and-Paste Layout
Literal cutting and pasting—physically cutting text and images and gluing them onto pages to create layouts. The traditional zine production method before digital design became common. Still used by many zinesters for its tactile quality and DIY aesthetic.
Master Copy
The original version of each page that you photocopy to make your print run. You create one master copy of the entire zine, then reproduce it.
Saddle Stitch
Binding method using staples through the spine of nested, folded pages. The standard zine binding. Called "saddle" stitch because the zine sits over the stapler like a saddle. See Technical Reference for details.
Perfect Binding
Binding method where pages are glued together at the spine, like a paperback book. Used for longer zines where stapling won't work. Requires special equipment or professional printing.
Risograph/Riso
A Japanese printing technology that creates vibrant, slightly imperfect prints in limited colors. Popular in art zine circles since the 2010s for affordable color printing. Has a distinctive aesthetic. More in Making Zines.
Print Run
The number of copies you make at one time. "I did a print run of 50" means you made 50 copies. Print runs for zines range from a handful to thousands, depending on audience and resources.
Colophon
Information about the zine's production—when and where it was made, print run size, tools used, etc. Often included at the end of art zines and more formal publications. Optional but nice for documentation.

Distribution and Community Terms

Distro
Short for distributor. A person or organization that sells zines on behalf of creators, usually taking a percentage of sales. Distros help zinesters reach audiences without handling all logistics themselves. See Scenes & Distribution.
Consignment
An arrangement where a shop or distro carries your zines and pays you when they sell. You provide inventory upfront, they sell it and send you money later (minus their cut). Requires trust and record-keeping.
Zine Fest
An event where zinesters gather to sell, trade, and celebrate zines. Can range from a dozen tables to hundreds. Usually includes tabling space, often includes workshops, readings, or other programming. More in Scenes & Distribution.
Tabling
Setting up a table at a zine fest or other event to sell or trade your zines. "I'm tabling at the zine fest next month" means you've reserved a table space to display your work.
Trade
Exchanging zines with other zinesters rather than buying/selling. A common practice that builds community and collections. "Want to trade?" is a frequent question at zine fests and in zine circles.
Scene
A network of people making, reading, and distributing zines, often in a specific geographic location or around a specific interest. "The Portland zine scene" or "the punk zine scene." See Scenes & Distribution.
DIY
Do It Yourself. The philosophy underlying zine culture: don't wait for permission or institutions, just make things yourself. Also describes the aesthetic of self-made, imperfect, handcrafted cultural production.
Mail Order
Selling/buying zines through the mail. The traditional distribution method before the internet. Still used, now often coordinated through email or social media but fulfilled through postal mail.
SASE
Self-Addressed Stamped Envelope. In mail-order culture, you'd send a SASE along with your order so the zinester could mail your zine back to you without paying postage. Less common now but still occasionally used.

Cultural and Historical Terms

Riot Grrrl
Feminist punk movement of the early 1990s that heavily used zines as a tool for consciousness-raising and community-building. Influential in establishing perzines as a major zine genre. See History & Culture.
Punk Zine
Zines connected to punk rock culture—documenting scenes, reviewing music, articulating punk philosophy. The punk connection is foundational to contemporary zine culture. More in Types & Genres.
Hardcore
A subgenre of punk (faster, more aggressive) with its own extensive zine culture. Also refers to being uncompromising or purist about DIY ethics—"hardcore DIY" means strictly independent, anti-commercial.
Zine Library
A collection of zines available for public access, either as a lending library or reference collection. Zine libraries preserve zine culture and make zines accessible. See Resources & Archives.
Factsheet Five / F5
The legendary zine review publication (1982-1998) that served as the central hub for discovering zines before the internet. Reviewed hundreds of zines per issue. Its death marked the end of an era. See Notable Zines.
Samizdat
Russian for "self-publishing." Refers to underground publication in Soviet Union and Eastern Bloc countries, where censorship forced writers to reproduce and distribute texts secretly. Important precursor to contemporary zine culture. See History & Culture.
Underground Comix
Self-published comics from the 1960s-70s that rejected mainstream comics industry. The "x" spelling distinguished them from commercial comics. Influential on zine culture and aesthetics.
Copy Culture
The use of photocopying technology for cultural production. Zines are part of copy culture—art and communication made possible by accessible duplication technology.

Specific Zine Types

Art Zine
A zine where visual art is the primary content. Can be collections of illustrations, prints, photographs, or experimental visual work. Often printed on higher-quality paper or using risograph. See Types & Genres.
Literary Zine
Zines that publish creative writing—poetry, fiction, essays. The DIY alternative to literary magazines. More in Types & Genres.
Music Zine
Zines focused on music scenes, bands, albums, shows. Includes reviews, interviews, scene documentation. The original zine type for many people. See Types & Genres.
Political Zine
Zines focused on political ideas, activism, social movements, or critique. Can range from single-issue advocacy to broad ideological perspectives. More in Types & Genres.
Travel Zine
Zines documenting travel experiences. Combine personal narrative with place-based observation. See Types & Genres.
How-To Zine
Instructional zines teaching skills or sharing practical information. Ranges from zine-making guides to bike repair to cooking to political organizing. More in Types & Genres.
Comic Zine
Zines that are primarily sequential art/comics. Overlaps with art zines but distinguished by narrative focus. Also called "minicomics" when they're small format. See Types & Genres.

Aesthetic and Style Terms

Cut-and-Paste Aesthetic
The distinctive visual style of physical collage and hand-assembled layout—different fonts, pasted images, visible edges, handwriting, imperfect alignment. Became so associated with zines that it's often replicated digitally.
Photocopied Look
The aesthetic qualities specific to photocopying—slightly degraded images, high contrast, occasional smudges or artifacts. Part of authentic zine aesthetic.
Lo-Fi
Low fidelity. Embracing low production values, rough quality, imperfect reproduction as aesthetic virtues rather than flaws. Central to DIY and zine philosophy.
Handmade
Emphasizing the physical labor and individual craft of zine production. Often used to describe zines with hand-drawn elements, hand-lettering, or physical collage.

Community and Cultural Concepts

Anti-Copyright
Explicitly rejecting copyright and encouraging free reproduction and distribution of work. Some zinesters print "anti-copyright" on their zines as a political statement against intellectual property. See Technical Reference for copyright discussion.
Creative Commons
A licensing system that allows creators to permit certain uses of their work while retaining some rights. Popular in zine culture for balancing open sharing with attribution.
Pay What You Can / PWYC
Sliding-scale pricing where buyers pay what they're able to afford, including nothing. Used by zinesters who want to make work accessible regardless of financial situation.
Free Pile / Free Box
At zine fests and shows, a communal area where people put zines and materials they're giving away for free. Take what you want, contribute what you can. Embodies gift economy aspects of zine culture.
Pen Pal
Someone you correspond with through letters. Zine culture has strong overlap with pen pal culture—people who trade zines often become pen pals. Mail correspondence creates different relationships than digital communication.
Scene Report
Writing about local scenes in zines, especially common in music zines. A way of documenting what's happening in different cities and connecting geographically dispersed scenes. See Notable Zines for Maximum Rocknroll's scene reports.

Attitudes and Philosophy

Authenticity
Highly valued in zine culture. Being genuine, honest, unfiltered, not performing for an audience. The imperfections and roughness of zines are seen as signs of authenticity—proof that real people made them without commercial mediation.
Gatekeepers
People or institutions who control access to media, publication, or cultural legitimacy. Zines explicitly reject gatekeepers—you don't need anyone's permission to make a zine. Anti-gatekeeper attitude is foundational to zine philosophy.
Sellout
Someone who compromises their principles for commercial success or mainstream acceptance. In punk and zine culture, remaining independent and uncommercial is valued. Being called a sellout is serious criticism, though definitions vary.
Keeping It Real
Staying true to your values, community, and authentic self rather than conforming to commercial or mainstream expectations. Related to authenticity and anti-sellout attitudes.
Support Your Local [X]
Ethic of supporting independent, local, DIY ventures rather than corporate alternatives. "Support your local zinester" means buy zines, go to zine fests, contribute to the scene. Community over commerce.

Slang and Informal Terms

Zine Haul
The collection of zines you acquire at a fest or through ordering. People post photos of their "zine hauls" on social media like treasure displays. Celebrating new acquisitions.
Zine Cred
Credibility or status within zine communities, earned through consistent participation, quality work, or longevity. Not formally recognized but informally understood. "They have serious zine cred" means they're established and respected.
Zine Burn
Fatigue from zine-making, tabling, or scene participation. Like any creative practice, zine-making can lead to burnout. Taking breaks is normal and healthy.
In The Mail / ITM
Announcement that you've mailed someone's zine order. "Your zine is ITM" means it's on its way. Part of mail-order culture's communication style.
Swap / Trade
Both verbs and nouns for exchanging zines. "Want to do a trade?" or "I'm open for swaps." Fundamental practice in zine community.
Reprint
When a zine sells out and you make more copies. "This is a reprint of issue 3" lets readers know it's not a new issue but a second (or third, etc.) printing of an older one.

Technical Production Terms

Bleed
Extra image area extending past the trim edge of a page. Necessary for images that run to the page edge in professional printing. Less relevant for photocopied zines. See Technical Reference.
DPI (Dots Per Inch)
Measurement of image resolution. 300 DPI is standard for printing. Lower resolution looks pixelated. More in Technical Reference.
Duplex Printing
Printing on both sides of paper automatically. Most modern photocopiers have duplex capability, making zine production much easier than manual double-siding. See Technical Reference.
Bone Folder
A bookbinding tool used to create crisp, sharp folds in paper. Traditionally made of bone, now often plastic. Not essential but helpful for clean folds. More in Technical Reference.
Long-Reach Stapler
A stapler designed to reach the center of folded pages for saddle-stitch binding. Essential tool for anyone making multiple folded zines. See Technical Reference.

Pronunciation Guide

Zine: "ZEEN" (rhymes with bean) or "ZINE" (rhymes with fine). Both are acceptable. Regional and generational differences exist. Don't police other people's pronunciation.
Perzine: "PER-zeen" (like personal + zine). Sometimes "PURR-zeen."
Risograph: "REE-so-graph" or "RYE-so-graph." Both heard, first is more common.
Aside: Using This Vocabulary Don't feel pressure to use all this terminology. Much of it emerges naturally as you participate in zine culture. Some people embrace the jargon, others avoid it as too insider-y. Use language that feels natural to you. The best zine communities welcome people regardless of how fluent they are in zine-speak. Asking "what does that mean?" is always acceptable and usually welcomed.
